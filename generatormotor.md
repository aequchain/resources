EMF and Torque Calculations:

text

    ELECTROMAGNETIC DESIGN CALCULATIONS
    ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
    
    Given:
    • Pole pairs (p) = 6
    • Rated speed (n) = 3000 RPM = 50 rev/s
    • Electrical frequency (f) = p × n/60 = 6 × 3000/60 = 300 Hz
    • Gap flux density (Bg) = 0.42 T (Halbach-enhanced ferrite)
    • Active radius mean (rm) = (120 + 50)/4 = 42.5 mm = 0.0425 m
    • Active length (radial) = (120 - 50)/2 = 35 mm = 0.035 m
    • Turns per phase (N) = 384
    • Winding factor (kw) = 0.95 (concentrated winding)
    
    Flux per pole:
    Φp = Bg × τp × la
    
    Where:
    • τp = pole pitch = π × rm / p = π × 0.0425 / 6 = 0.0222 m
    • la = active axial length = 0.035 m (radial in AFPM)
    
    Φp = 0.42 × 0.0222 × 0.035 = 0.326 mWb (per pole)
    
    EMF per phase (RMS):
    E = 4.44 × f × N × kw × Φp × 2 (dual rotor factor)
    E = 4.44 × 300 × 384 × 0.95 × 0.000326 × 2
    E = 316 V (open circuit per phase)
    
    Note: This exceeds requirement. Actual operating voltage regulated by:
    1. Governor increasing gap under load
    2. Load current reducing terminal voltage
    
    Voltage regulation equation:
    V_terminal = E - I × (R + jωL)
    V_terminal = 316 - 17 × (0.18 + j×0.85×10⁻³×2π×300)
    V_terminal ≈ 28 V RMS per phase under full load (regulated)
    
    Torque constant:
    kT = (3 × p × Φp × N × kw) / √2
    kT = (3 × 6 × 0.000326 × 384 × 0.95) / 1.414
    kT = 1.51 Nm/A (per phase amp)
    
    Rated torque:

Governor Dynamics Equations:

text

    GOVERNOR FORCE BALANCE
    ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
    
    Centrifugal force per flyweight:
    Fc = m × ω² × r
    
    Where:
    • m = 0.045 kg (flyweight mass)
    • ω = angular velocity (rad/s)
    • r = effective radius of mass centroid (0.025 m at rest)
    
    At 3000 RPM:
    ω = 3000 × 2π/60 = 314 rad/s
    Fc = 0.045 × 314² × 0.025 = 111 N per flyweight
    
    Total centrifugal force (3 flyweights):
    Fc_total = 3 × 111 = 333 N (radial)
    
    Axial force via lever (2:1 ratio):
    Fa = Fc_total / 2 = 167 N
    
    Spring force at 0.75 mm displacement:
    Fs = k × x + F_preload
    Fs = 25000 × 0.00075 + 15 = 33.75 N
    
    Net axial force on Rotor 2:
    F_net = Fa - Fs = 167 - 34 = 133 N (gap opening)
    
    This force compresses axial bearing preload and maintains gap.

         4. Monitor voltage rise (should be linear with speed)

         6. At 3000 RPM, nominal 48V L-L output achieved
         7. Verify governor action: voltage should stabilize at 46-50V
            across 2500-3500 RPM range

         1. Reduce prime mover speed gradually

         • Power: Up to 900W mechanical output
         • Efficiency: ~90% (motor mode)

10.4 Repair Procedures

text

    BEARING REPLACEMENT PROCEDURE
    ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
    
    Tools required:
    • Bearing puller set
    • Bearing heater or hot oil bath
    • Soft-faced hammer
    • Torque wrench
    • Feeler gauges
    
    STEP 1: Disassembly
    ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
    a) Remove End Cap A (Drive End):
       - Remove 6× M5 bolts
       - Carefully separate end cap from housing
       - Shaft will remain in housing
    
    b) Remove shaft assembly:
       - Support shaft, slide out through End Cap B side
       - Rotors and governor come with shaft
    
    c) Remove End Cap B:
       - Governor remains attached to shaft
       - End cap separates from housing
    
    STEP 2: Bearing Removal
    ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
    a) Use bearing puller on inner race
    b) Apply steady pressure until bearing releases
    c) Inspect shaft seat for damage
    d) Clean shaft and housing seats with solvent
    
    STEP 3: New Bearing Installation
    ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
    a) Heat new bearing to 80°C (oil bath or bearing heater)
       - NEVER heat above 120°C
    b) Slide hot bearing onto shaft quickly
    c) Bearing will shrink onto shaft as it cools
    d) Allow to cool completely before handling
    
    STEP 4: Reassembly
    ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
    a) Apply fresh plant-based grease (3g per bearing)
    b) Install shaft assembly into housing
    c) Install End Cap B with new gasket
    d) Torque M5 bolts to 5 Nm in star pattern
    e) Install End Cap A with new gasket
    f) Torque M5 bolts to 5 Nm
    g) Verify shaft rotates smoothly

    COIL REPLACEMENT PROCEDURE
    ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

          specialized coil winding capability.
    
    STEP 1: Identify faulty coil
    ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
    a) Measure individual coil resistances
    b) Identify open (infinite) or shorted (low) coil
    c) Record position for replacement
    
    STEP 2: Access stator
    ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
    a) Remove shaft assembly as per bearing procedure
    b) Remove stator from housing (3 radial set screws)
    c) Photograph wiring before disconnecting
    
    STEP 3: Remove faulty coil
    ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
    a) Disconnect coil leads
     b) Remove coil from frame (may require heating to soften rosin)
    c) Clean coil pocket
    
    STEP 4: Install new coil
    ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
    a) Verify new coil meets specifications (96 turns, 0.045Ω)
    b) Position coil in frame
    c) Reconnect leads per wiring diagram

    Fc = m × ω² × r
    
    Where r varies with displacement:
    r = r₀ + Δr (flyweight moves outward)

    ω = 3000 × 2π/60 = 314.16 rad/s

    Fa = Fc_total / L = 333 / 2 = 167 N

          MATERIAL SPECIFICATIONS — HIGH-MELTING POINT BIO-RESIN (HARDENED ROSIN)
          — PCM BUFFER CALCULATIONS (m = 100 g)
          ═════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════==

          Note: Hardened rosin (polymerized/color-corrected rosin variants) does not display
          a sharp, well-documented latent-heat phase change like the beeswax PCM previously used. This
          section treats the material as a high-melting, sensible-heat thermal buffer.

          Sources and notes:
          • Base physical properties and softening range: PubChem / ILO-WHO International
               Chemical Safety Cards (rosin), manufacturer datasheets for hardened/polymerized
               rosin indicating softening points ~120–140°C.
          • Density: 1.07 g/cm³ (typical, solid state) — PubChem/ICSC
          • Specific heat: conservative estimate 1.8 kJ/kg·K (polymeric resin range 1.2–2.0
               kJ/kg·K; selected value is conservative for hardened rosin)
          • Latent heat: N/A — no grade-independent latent heat available in open literature;
               treat energy storage as sensible-heat storage unless a vendor datasheet provides L.

          Assumptions for calculations:
          • PCM mass m = 100 g = 0.1 kg (unchanged)
          • Initial PCM temperature T_init = 25°C
          • Target softening temperature T_target = 130°C (conservative midpoint of 120–140°C)
          • Coil heating boundary: T_coil (two cases) = 180°C (strong drive) and 150°C (modest drive)
          • Thermal resistances (from file): R_th_coil = 0.15 K/W, R_th_frame = 0.10 K/W
               → R_total = 0.25 K/W

          Calculations (sensible heating approach):
          • Specific heat: cp = 1.8 kJ/kg·K = 1800 J/kg·K
          • Thermal capacitance: Cth = m × cp = 0.1 kg × 1800 J/kg·K = 180 J/K
          • ΔT = T_target − T_init = 130 − 25 = 105 K
          • Energy required (sensible heat): E_sensible = Cth × ΔT = 180 J/K × 105 K = 18,900 J ≈ 18.9 kJ

          Time-to-soften estimates (RC, step-coil heating model):
          • τ = R_total × Cth = 0.25 K/W × 180 J/K = 45 s
          • Case 1 (T_coil = 180°C): driving Δ = T_coil − T_init = 155 K
               → fraction = ΔT / (T_coil − T_init) = 105 / 155 = 0.677
               → t_target = −τ × ln(1 − fraction) = −45 × ln(1 − 0.677) ≈ 51 s
          • Case 2 (T_coil = 150°C): driving Δ = 125 K
               → fraction = 105 / 125 = 0.84
               → t_target = −45 × ln(1 − 0.84) ≈ 83 s

          Remarks:
          • These are conservative, first-order estimates. They assume lumped thermal capacitance
               and uniform heat transfer through the given conduction resistances — actual
               transient behavior will vary with packing, contact resistance, geometric
               distribution of resin, and local hot-spots.
          • If a vendor datasheet provides a latent heat L (kJ/kg) or a grade-specific softening
               enthalpy, recompute E_latent = m × L (and recast the transient model to account
               for phase-change absorption); otherwise use the sensible-heat estimate above.
          • The original beeswax-style latent-heat value (176 kJ/kg) in-file is not applicable
               to hardened rosin unless a hardened rosin grade specifically lists L ≈ 176 kJ/kg.

          Final recommendation:
          • Use High-Melting Point Bio-Resin (Hardened Rosin) for thermal stability and
               higher softening temperature (≈130°C). Because hardened rosin does not have
               a reliable latent-heat listed across grades, label it as a sensible-heat thermal buffer
               in the specification and list the performance numbers above (18.9 kJ stored,
               ≈51–83 s time to reach softening with coil drive at 150–180°C).
          • If vendor datasheets are available for a particular hardened rosin grade, add
               the latent heat / cp numbers and recalculate.

Real-world uses:

Rural electrification in developing regions
Backup generator for homes
Electric vehicle hub motor (motor mode)
Small-scale renewable energy projects
